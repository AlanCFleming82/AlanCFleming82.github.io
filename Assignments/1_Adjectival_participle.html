Adjectival participles

Adjectival participles are those participles which perform the same functions as an adjective. These functions include the attributive adjective, the substantive adjective, and the predicate adjective.

Like all participles, the attributive participle expresses a time relative to the primary verb in the clause and it may be accompanied by an adverb, object, or prepositional phrase. Generally, present participles may be translated into English as -ing forms whereas past participles may be translated as -ed (or equivalent) forms. Future active participles are generally translated as “about to” or “going to”. Often the attributive participle is best translated into English as a relative clause. Consider the following phrases including adjectival participles:

Exercitus fugiēns may be translated as “fleeing army” or “army which is/was fleeing”.

Sprēta pietās may be translated as “spurned piety” or “piety which was/had been spurned”.

Nōmen mansūrum may be translated as “a name about to last” or “a name which will/would last” or sometimes “a name destined to last”.

Like the attributive adjective, an adjectival participle may simply ascribe a quality to a noun in a sentence.

Mīsērunt lēgātōs minantēs Rōmam.

“They sent legates who were threatening Rome.”

Hīc Morbī pallentēs et trīstis Senectūs habitant.

“Here the pallid Diseases and sad Old age dwells.”

Moenia fabricāta manū Neptūnī cecidērunt.

“The walls, which were built by the hand of Neptune, fell.”

Alexandrīnae nāvēs secutūrae classis adventum nūntiant.

“The Alexandrian ships announce the arrival of the fleet which will follow.”

When a present participle functions as an attributive adjective in the ablative singular, the ending is usually -ī (ie. ducentī, agentī), sharing a form with the dative singular.

Mē trementī vōce exsulem appellāvit.

“He called me an exile with his voice shaking.”

An attributive participle can also function as a noun in a sentence. This substantive use of the participle modifies a generic noun (a man, women, things, etc.) or an unexpressed noun which can easily be supplied from the context. In translating, we must supply the unexpressed generic noun depending on form and context, for example: “those listening” (audientēs), “the women who were sought” (petītae), or “things heard” (audīta). A substantive participle can be easily recognized because it does not modify a noun in the sentence.

Sagittīs fugientēs persequī prohibentur.

“They are prevented from pursuing those who are fleeing by arrows.”

Lūx ultima vīctōs vocat.

“A final day summons those defeated.”

Inter peritūra vīvimus.

“We live amid things destined to perish.”

When a present participle functions as a substantive in the ablative singular, the ending is usually -e (ie. dūcente, agente).

Nēmō beneficium ā nesciente accipit.

“No one can receive a benefit from one who is unaware.”

Sometimes an adjectival participle will appear as a predicate adjective. In such cases, the participle accompanies a linking verb and ascribes a state or quality to the subject of the verb.

Mundus est animāns et compōs ratiōnis.

“The universe is alive and in possession of reason.”

Ibi nunc Neāpolis sita est.

“Neapolis is now situated there.”

Sometimes these will look identical to the perfect or pluperfect passive, and in such instances context must be used to differentiate between these two meanings. For example, taken as a predicate adjective locāta est means “it is situated” while taken as a perfect passive verb it means “it was placed”.

Some additional notes

An ablative paired with an adjectival participles often appears expressing means, manner, characteristic, etc. These examples differ from the ablative absolute (see Lesson 2) in that they perform functions of the ablative, rather than those of the adverbial participle.

Mīlitēs pilīs missīs hostium phalangem perfrēgērunt.

“Our soldiers broke their phalanx by means of thrown javalins.” (means)

Salmacis versō gradū discēdere simulat.

“Salmacis pretended to depart with a reversed step.” (manner)

Mē frāctō animō esse loquēbantur.

“They were saying that I was of a broken mind.” (characteristic)

Many participles are so closely associated with their adjectival function that have essentially become adjectives. Some examples include: sapiēns, volēns, prōmissus, patiēns, doctus, futūrus.

When these same forms would have an object, they regularly take an objective genitive (see Lesson 4) rather than an accusative direct object (see Woodcock §97).

Alphēos longī labōris patiēns erat.

“Alpheus was hardy enough for this long labor.”

Ille pāce factā numquam amāns cruōris erat.

“He was never desirous of slaughter after peace was made.”

The future passive participle is rarely found outside of set constructions (ie. gerundive, periphrastic conjugation, etc.). When it does appear as an adjectival participle, it is best translated as “suited to be”, “needing to be”, or “worth being”.

Brūtus librōs dē iūre legendōs scrīpsit.

“Brutus wrote books about law which are worth reading.” (literally, “worth being read”)

Sometimes adjectival participles may be made comparative, superlative, or adverbial according to the usual rules for adjectives.

Nōs parātiōrēs reperiet quam putābat.

“He will find us readier than he supposed.”

Eius sermō inquinātissimus et blanditiae flāgitiōsae valuērunt.

“His utterly tainted speech and blatant pandering prevailed.”

Multa minanter agat.

“He does many things in a threatening way.”

[eram nescius here introduces an indirect question]

[Capitōliō, “Capitol” or “Capitoline hill”]

[fistula, here “a water pipe”; faciem arcūs, here “appearance of a rainbow”]

[ut, here “as”; furientium, “be insane”; acta, here “drawn” modifying suspiria]
